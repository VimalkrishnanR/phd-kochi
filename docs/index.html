Through the Tomes of Shodhganga

Kochi, The Queen of the Arabian Sea and her 'Subjects' of Research

By Vimal Krishnan R

July 13th, 2026

'The whole PhD system to me is an abomination. I don't have a PhD myself, I feel myself very lucky I didn't have to go through it. I think it's a gross distortion of the educational process', said Freeman Dyson, the renowned physicist and mathematician, in an interview recorded years back. Dyson disapproved of how the PhD system 'imposed' a 'straightjacket' and the 'same constraints' on all students. He himself preferred to work intensively on various problems, rather than just one, for time spans that didn't fit neatly into standard PhD cycles. Today, however, the PhD is prized, especially in India. Academia here encourages a 'publish and flourish' culture (or should I say 'publish or perish?') in the race for rankings.One is expected to publish interminably, both during their PhD and beyond. It is in this context that Shodhganga stands out as a towering antithesis to Dyson's opinion.

Shodhganga is an online database that gives open access to thousands of PhD theses submitted to various Indian universities. In itself, this vision of making knowledge freely accessible is commendable. But amid a frenzy of rankings, accreditations and shortsighted metrics evaluating academia, such initiatives are perhaps getting 'distorted'. Shodhganga's website calls itself a 'reservoir of Indian theses' and the homepage proclaims reaching a milestone of the '6,00,000th thesis' upload. A stopwatch-style counter on the homepage (please click on the link and see for yourself!) displays the number of 'full text theses', 'synopses' uploaded, the number of contributing universities, and more. There is also a bar chart that shows the number of 'theses submitted' in the last 6 months and a leaderboard showing the top 10 contributing universities. I wouldn't be surpirsed if in the near future, quarterly updates are included! The urgency is clear! Meditations on the Gita and the Shanta Rasa are all racing to find a place among Shodhganga's portals. I wonder what Dyson would have made of this ever-expanding and incorporeal tree of knowledge. Perhaps he would have quoted this description—'the feverish Library, whose random volumes constantly threaten to transmogrify into others, so that they affirm all things, deny all things, and confound and confuse all things, like some mad and hallucinating deity'—from 'The Library of Babel', a short story by Jorge Luis Borges where he presents a fascinating endless library. But Borges also describes this labyrinthine space as the 'total library', one that contains all knowledge. In this infinite library where books are arranged in hexagonal galleries: 'there [is] no personal problem, no world problem, whose eloquent solution [does] not exist—somewhere in some hexagon'.

As an academic, I too am entangled in the publish-and-survive race. Lured by the infinities of Shodhganga, I scraped and compiled a list of PhD thesis titles that include the interchangeably used toponyms of my home city in central Kerala: 'Ernakulam', 'Kochi', or 'Cochin'—just to indulge my curiosity about how this region is being researched. Here, I offer a brief and preliminary data exploration of the academic scholarship framing this historic port city, the Queen of the Arabian Sea!

Science leads the race

When I searched Shodhganga using the toponyms as keywords, I expected a voluminous dataset of thousands of thesis titles. In an ideal academic setting where quality, depth, and meaning would trump quantity, the number of PhD theses on a topic would not matter. But for now, I must collude in the number game. I obtained 350 thesis titles. An analysis of this corpus reveals that studies related to 'Science and Environment' are in the lead:

There are several studies on the Cochin estuary, the Kochi coastal tract, the wetland species of Ernakulam, and a range of other scientific topics. Also prevalent are studies on social and development-related aspects like health and wellbeing as well as on the region's business and economy, especially focusing on the Cochin Port and special economic zone. These domains consitute almost 90% of the research. Taking the baton from the Romans, the Portuguese, and the Dutch, our academics continue to concentrate on the ecological and economic facets of this historic coast!

For Kolkata, a comparable historic port city, Shodhganga returns close to a thousand thesis titles—if numbers are a proxy for research diversity! Through this comparison, I intend to convey one critical aspect of the data: Kochi remains a deeply understudied region, where the existing scholarship—though significant—seems to cluster around a few dominant themes. This glaring gap is also invitation for greater research diversity. I reiterate: by 'diversity', I am emphasising the need for deeper, high-quality research that opens up new intellectual avenues, rather than merely stacking up thesis counts. Kochi is a thriving hub of the contemporary art and film industry, the venue of an international biennale, and home to a wealth of built heritage. There are also hidden treasures, like the sculptures at the Subash Bose Park that were crafted during the International Sculpture Symposium of 1990—considered by many as a precursor to the biennale. Despite its rich historical legacy and enduring cultural habitat, studies exploring these facets of Kochi are remarkably sparse. Kochi has other distinctions. It is the only city in India featuring a water metro system and it has the world's first solar powered airport.Yet, research on public transport is virtually non-existent. Moreover, as the Government of Kerala scrambles to execute policy measures to address challenges like declining student intake at state universities and an alarming brain drain, there is an urgent need for a greater degree of critical research and reflection on the higher education ecosystem both in Kerala and within Kochi. A point to ponder: the statistics on Shodhganga's homepage celebrate the quantity of outputs. Can this national portal also offer constructive meta-critical insights, foregrounding the regional potential of various research topics and local institutional frameworks?

Such palimpsets of data on Shodhganga, awaiting excavation, bring to the fore the untapped potential for many different strands of innovative research. As several respected academics have discussed in this erudite collection of essays, one task of scholars here is to actively configure cultural difference and generate new research perspectives emanating from non-European locations.

If Alexander perceived the 'Ganges' as merely a 'river', to be marked linearly on a map, the Janapadas of India held a significantly different non-linear view of 'Ganga', seeing her as an all-encompassing rain terrain. Shodhganga can indeed transform into a 'lively archive' of regional research perspectives, capturing the biocultural diversities of Kochi, Kerala, and India! I end with a fun chronological fact. A thesis titled 'Cochin Under the Portuguese and the Dutch' by one Okon L. Abraham, completed in 1930, is the oldest in my dataset! Here is the first sentence in the first chapter of this fascinating document:

'By Cochin may be meant either the native Indian State of that name or the chief town of that state together with a small plot of British territory adjoining to it.'

At the Mattancherry Palace—built by the Portuguese in 1555 and restored by the Dutch in 1665—are the breathtaking Ramayana murals depicting the birth of Daśaratha's progenies Rāma, Lakṣmaṇa, Bharata, and Śatrughna. They are demanding attention—from our scholars! And it only costs ₹5 entry fee to see them!

Methodology: I scraped thesis titles containing the toponyms 'Ernakulam', 'Cochin', and 'Kochi' available on Shodhganga and consolidated them into a single dataset. After removing duplicate entries, I manually coded the subcategories(topics) of the titles. These subcategories were then grouped into defined superordinate categories using an AI model. After training the model on a random sample of the data, I evaluated its accuracy using a confusion matrix. Finally, the AI model classified all subcategories into superordinate categories, which I further refined manually. I used Python and Pandas to clean and analyse the data and visualised the data using Datawrapper, Flourish, RawGraph, and Adobe Illustrator.

Note: The dataset excludes thesis titles that do not contain one of the three toponyms. This story acknowledges that there could be other research studies that concentrate on this region without including the region's name in their titles. This dataset has been selected based on the assumption that thesis titles that explicitly include the region's name are those most likely to feature it as the primary focal point.
