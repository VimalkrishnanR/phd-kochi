Through the Tomes of Shodhganga

The Queen of the Arabian Sea as a 'Subject' of Research

By Vimal Krishnan R

July 13th, 2026

'The whole PhD system to me is an abomination. I don't have a PhD myself, I feel myself very lucky I didn't have to go through it. I think it's a gross distortion of the educational process', said Freeman Dyson, the renowned physicist and mathematician, in an interview recorded years back. Dyson disapproved of how the PhD system 'imposed' a 'straightjacket' and the same constraints on all students. He himself preferred to work intensively on various problems, rather than just one, for time spans that didn't fit neatly into the standard PhD cycle. Today, however, the PhD is prized, as academia encourages a 'publish and flourish' culture (or should I say 'publish or perish?') in the race for rankings. It is in this context that Shodhganga stands out as a towering antithesis to Dyson's opinion.

Shodhganga is an online database that gives open access to thousands of PhD theses submitted to various Indian universities. In itself, this vision of making knowledge freely accessible is commendable. But amid a frenzy of rankings, accreditations and shortsighted metrics evaluating academia, such initiatives are perhaps getting 'distorted'. Shodhganga's website calls itself a 'reservoir of Indian theses' and the homepage proclaims reaching a milestone of the '6,00,000th thesis' upload. A stopwatch-style counter on the homepage (please click on the link and see for yourself!) displays the number of theses and synopses uploaded, the number of contributing universities, and more. The urgency is clear! Dissertations on Aurelius' 'Meditations' and Kundera's 'Slowness' are all racing to find a place among Shodhganga's portals. I wonder what Dyson would have made of this ever-expanding and incorporeal tree of knowledge. Perhaps he would have quoted this description—'the feverish Library, whose random volumes constantly threaten to transmogrify into others, so that they affirm all things, deny all things, and confound and confuse all things, like some mad and hallucinating deity'—from 'The Library of Babel', a short story by Jorge Luis Borges where he presents a fascinating endless library. But Borges also describes this labyrinthine space as the 'total library', one that contains all knowledge. In this infinite library where books are arranged in hexagonal galleries: 'there [is] no personal problem, no world problem, whose eloquent solution [does] not exist—somewhere in some hexagon'.

As an academic with the prized 'Dr.' prefix, I too am in the race to publish and find new research ideas. Intrigued by the infinities of Shodhganga, I scraped and compiled a list of PhD thesis titles that contained the toponyms 'Ernakulam', 'Kochi', or 'Cochin', just to indulge my curiosity about how my home city is being researched. Here, I explore the 'academic hexagons' framing this historic port city, the Queen of the Arabian Sea!

The Dominant Academic Narratives

When I searched Shodhganga using the toponyms as keywords, I expected a voluminous dataset of thousands of thesis titles. In an ideal academic setting where quality, depth, and meaning would trump quantity, the number of PhD theses on a topic would not matter. But for now, I must collude in the number game. I obtained 350 thesis titles. An analysis of this corpus reveals how studies related to 'Science and Environment' are the highest:

There are several studies on the Cochin estuary, the Kochi coastal tract, the wetland species of Ernakulam, and a range of other scientific topics. Studies on social demographics like health and wellbeing as well as on the region's economy, especially featuring the Cochin Port, are also prevalent. These domains consitute almost 90% of the research. Taking the baton from the Romans, the Portuguese and the Dutch, our academicians continue to be captivated by this historic coast!

The diagram below gives a snapshot of how academics are viewing this historic region from various vantage points. Though each of them is undeniably significant, this city certainly provides fodder for more diverse research angles. For a comparable city like Kolkata, Shodhganga returns close to a 1000 thesis titles—if numbers are a proxy for research diversity! Despite Kochi being a thriving hub of the contemporary art and film industry, despite its rich historical roots and enduring cultural habitat, studies exploring these facets are remarkably sparse. Moreover, as the Government of Kerala scrambles to execute policy measures to address systemic challenges like declining student intake at state universities and a growing egress of the student population, there is an urgent need for more critical research and reflection on the higher education ecosystem both in Kerala and within Kochi.

The data clearly reveals the untapped potential for more collective reflection and innovative research. As several respected academics have discussed in this erudite collection of essays, the task of scholars here is to actively configure cultural difference and generate new research perspectives emanating from non-European locations.

If Alexander conceived the 'Ganges' as merely a river to be mapped, the Janapadas of India held a significantly different view of 'Ganga', seeing her as an all-encompassing rain terrain. Shodhganga can indeed transform into a 'lively archive' of regional research perspectives, capturing the biocultural diversities of Ernakulam, Kerala, and India! I end with a fun chronological fact. A thesis titled 'Cochin Under the Portuguese and the Dutch' by one Okon L. Abraham, completed in 1930, is the oldest in my dataset! Here is the first sentence in the first chapter of this fascinating document:

'By Cochin may be meant either the native Indian State of that name or the chief town of that state together with a small plot of British territory adjoining to it.'

Methodology: I scraped theses titles containing the toponyms 'Ernakulam', 'Cochin', and 'Kochi' available on Shodhganga and consolidated these into a single dataset. After removing duplicate entries, I manually coded the subcategories of the thesis titles. Then, the sub-categories were grouped into superordinate categories using an AI model. After training the model on a random sample of the subcategories, its performance was evaluated using a confusion matrix. Then, the sub-categories in the complete dataset was classified into superordinate categories by the AI model, which was further refined manually.
