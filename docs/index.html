Through the Tomes of Shodhganga

The Queen of the Arabian Sea: A 'Subject' of Research

By Vimal Krishnan R

July 13th, 2026

'The whole PhD system to me is an abomination. I don't have a PhD myself, I feel myself very lucky I didn't have to go through it. I think it's a gross distortion of the educational process', said Freeman Dyson, the renowned physicist and mathematician, in an interview recorded years back. Dyson was referring to the PhD cycle that 'straightjacketed' students and 'condemned' them to work on a single problem for several years. Today, however, the PhD is prized, as academia encourages a 'publish and flourish' culture (or should I say 'publish or perish?') in the race for rankings. It is in this context that Shodhganga stands as a towering antithesis to Dyson's opinion.

Shodhganga is an open online database that gives access to thousands of PhD theses submitted to various Indian universities. In itself, this vision of making knowledge freely accessible is commendable. But amid a frenzy of rankings, accreditations and shortsighted metrics evaluating academia, such initiatives are perhaps getting 'distorted'. Shodhganga's website calls itself a 'reservoir of Indian theses' and the homepage proclaims reaching a milestone of the '6,00,000th thesis' upload. A stopwatch-style counter on the homepage (Please click on the link and see for yourself!) displays the number of theses and synopses uploaded, the number of contributing universities, and more. The urgency is clear! Aurelius' 'Meditations' and Kundera's 'Slowness' are all racing to be packaged into dissertations. I wonder what Dyson would have made of this ever-expanding and incorporeal tree of knowledge! Perhaps he would have quoted this description—'the feverish Library, whose random volumes constantly threaten to transmogrify into others, so that they affirm all things, deny all things, and confound and confuse all things, like some mad and hallucinating deity'—from 'The Library of Babel', a short story by Jorge Luis Borges where he presents a fascinating endless library. But Borges also describes this labyrinthine space as the 'total library', one that contains all knowledge. In this library where books are arranged in hexagonal galleries: 'there [is] no personal problem, no world problem, whose eloquent solution [does] not exist—somewhere in some hexagon'.

As an academic with the prized 'Dr.' prefix, I too am in the race to publish. Intrigued by the infinities of Shodhganga, I scraped and compiled a list of PhD thesis titles that contained the toponyms 'Ernakulam', 'Kochi', or 'Cochin', just to indulge my curiosity about how my home city is being researched. Here, I explore the 'academic hexagons' framing this historic port city, the Queen of the Arabian Sea!

The Dominant Academic Narratives

When I searched Shodhganga using the toponyms as keywords, I expected a voluminous dataset containing thousands of thesis titles. However, I got only around 350. An analysis of this corpus reveals how studies related to 'Science and Environment' are the highest:

There are several studies on the Cochin estuary, the Kochi coastal tract, the wetland species of Ernakulam, and a range of other scientific topics. Studies on social demographics like health and wellbeing as well as on the region's economy, especially featuring the Cochin Port, are also prevalent. Taking the baton from the Romans, the Portuguese and the Dutch, our academicians continue to be captivated by this historic coast!

There are a few studies focussing on education in this region, especially higher education. For a district in a state that prides itself with a very high rate of literacy, there is a lot of scope for critical research and reflections on education. And despite having a thriving film and art sector and a rich history, research exploring these aspects of Kochi are less. A literary masterpiece like the Aitheehyamala contains several references to various temples, geographical markers, and living cultures within Ernakulam. The data reveals that there is ample scope for exploring such research topics. Ideally, such topics ought to be studied at universities here, rather than an Indology department abroad. If Shodhganga can transform itself into a lively archive of such clusters of research topics on the scientific, socio-economic, and perfomative annd mnemocultures of various regions in India, that would truly be remarkable.
